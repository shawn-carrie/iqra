<h2>1. Introduction to Arabic</h2>

<h3>1.1 What is Arabic?</h3>

<p>Overview</p>

<ul>

<li>Arabic has many dialects, as well as diglossia</li>

<li>Arabic dialects vary greatly between regions, countries, cities, and even neighborhoods</li>

<li> </li>

</ul>

<p> </p>

<p>Arabic is not just one language - there are many different dialects in the many different places where Arabic is spoken.</p>

<p><strong>Modern Standard Arabic </strong>is the common language…</p>

<p><em>You may be told that Modern Standard Arabic is the official language of 22 countries as well as the United Nations. That doesn’t mean that anybody speaks it.</em></p>

<p><strong>Dialectal Arabic</strong> refers to the spoken language which is used every day.</p>

<ul>

<li>It is not standardized, and is not homogeneous within a country. Dialects can vary even by city or neighborhood - just like in other languages! <em>There are variations between the regions of course, as Arabic is spoken on a dialectic continuum throughout the Middle East and North Africa.</em></li>

</ul>

<p>Egyptian</p>

<p>Levantine (also known as Eastern Arabic or Mashriqi Arabic)</p>

<p>Syria</p>

<p>Lebanon</p>

<p>Jordan</p>

<p>Palestine and Israel</p>

<p>Gulf Arabic</p>

<p>North African Arabic</p>

<p>Other African Arabic Dialects</p>

<p><em>Egypt has long been a cultural center of the Arabic-speaking world, producing a lot of TV shows, films, and music which are widely known to Arabic speakers. The Levant has also produced some well known and timeless cultural icons, making it also widely known and understood. It’s not as widely understood as Egyptian Arabic, but its influence is increasing thanks to pop music and TV dramas.</em></p>

<p><em>Other Arabic dialects are not as well understood…</em></p>

<p> </p>

<p>El-Said Badawi, a prominent Arab linguist and former director of CASA, classified Arabic into five different levels:</p>

<p>فصحى التراث, “Classical Arabic;”</p>

<p>فصحى المعاصر, “Modern Standard Arabic;”</p>

<p>عامية المثقفين, “colloquial of the well-educated,”</p>

<p>عامية المتنورين, “colloquial of the basically educated;” and</p>

<p>عامية الأميين, “colloquial of the illiterates.”</p>

<p>The “neatness” of all five levels, he noted, is “achievable only through the highest level of abstraction.” (See Badawi’s article and book). As Badawi understood, most colloquial forms contain micro-variations that depend on the speaker’s geographic origins, socio-economic “class,” educational level, and subject matter, all of which overlap and defy simple categorization into discrete sets. Further complicating matters, native speakers commonly switch between higher and lower forms, sometimes mid-sentence, depending on context and desired effect.</p>

<p>Nevertheless, in order to help teachers and students distinguish educated from less educated colloquial forms, we’ve distinguished some content as Educated Spoken Arabic (ESA). Overlapping with both MSA and multiple dialects simultaneously, ESA has the advantages of lending relative ease to conversation, bestowing prestige on the speaker, and being widely understood. It’s commonly used in classrooms and for discussions about intellectual topics (such as politics, society, history, culture, religion, etc.) as well as for facilitating communication among native speakers of different dialects.</p>

<h3>1.2 Dialects</h3>

<p>Arabic dialects vary greatly between regions, countries, cities, and even neighborhoods</p>

<p>Luckily - dialects are much simpler grammatically!</p>

<h3>1.3 How is Arabic similar / different?</h3>

<p><strong>Different</strong></p>

<ul>

<li>Arabic exhibits <em>diglossia</em></li>

<li>Arabic is written right to left</li>

<li>Arabic has case endings</li>

</ul>

<p><strong>Similar</strong></p>

<ul>

<li>Builds words by construction</li>

<li>One letter = one sound (unlike English, similar to …)</li>

</ul>

<p>Grammar Terms to know:</p>

<p>Knowing grammar terms in [your first language] will help in learning a new language.</p>

<p>You can come back to this at any time.</p>

<h2> </h2>

<h1>1.5 Grammar Foundations</h1>

<h2>The root system</h2>

<p>Most Arabic words have a triliteral root (made up of three consonants or radicals), and it is this root that defines the underlying meaning of the word. Thus, words which are derived from the same root will frequently have a shared basic meaning.</p>

<p>We can see how the consonantal root system works using the root ك ت ب (k t b) where words derived from this root have a meaning linked to ‘writing’:</p>

<table>

<tbody>

<tr>

<td>

<p dir="rtl">كَتبََ</p>

</td>

<td>kataba</td>

<td>he wrote</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>

<p dir="rtl">مَكْتبٌَ</p>

</td>

<td>maktabun</td>

<td>desk / office</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>

<p dir="rtl">مَكتبَةٌَ</p>

</td>

<td>maktabatun</td>

<td>library</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>

<p dir="rtl">كاتبٌِ</p>

</td>

<td>kātibun</td>

<td>author</td>

</tr>

</tbody>

</table>

<p>The different but related words are formed by adding vowels and affixes to the root following specific patterns or forms which give predictable meanings. In other words, by adding certain vowels and affixes to the root, particular noun or verb patterns are reproduced, which may lead to a vast number of words which are predictable in both form and meaning. When you come across a new word, assuming you recognise the root, you can approximate the basic meaning of the word. It is easy to see how knowing the root and understanding the root patterns enables the learner to understand and access a plethora of words.</p>

<p>There are instances, however, where one root can have two different meanings (which might have originally been linked) and either each Form applies to both meanings or each Form produces a different meaning. The root س و س (s w s), for example, carries both the meaning ‘to govern or dominate’ and ‘to rot or decay’. From an etymological perspective it would be interesting to know whether these two meanings were once semantically linked: a comment on the inherent corruption of government, perhaps? Other interesting, thought-provoking examples are تشََفَّ ى (tashaffa), which means both ‘to heal’ and ‘to take revenge’; and the root م ن و (m n w), which gives both the wordsأمنیةّ ٌ (umniyatun) ‘a wish or desire’ and مِنىً (minan) ‘sperm’.</p>

<p>These facets enable countless ways to play with words, suggested meanings, double entendres, and give Arabic a richness which is a delight to discover.</p>

<h2>Types of Sentences: The إضافة (adjective phrase) and the equational sentence</h2>

<p>The إضافة construction is described as consisting of two nouns with a special relationship, like غرفة جلوس or عاصمة سوریا, etc.</p>

<p>It is important to distinguish this construction from noun-adjective phrases and certain noun-noun and noun-adjective combinations that function as full sentences. The following examples illustrate the difference between these three types of structure:</p>

<table>

<tbody>

<tr>

<td>

</td>

<td>the country's capital, the capital of the country</td>

</tr>

<td>

<p dir="rtl">المدینة الكبیرة</p>

<td>the big city</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>indefinite</td>

<td>

<p dir="rtl">مدینة كبیرة</p>

</td>

<td>a big city</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>Complete (equational/nominal) sentence</td>
